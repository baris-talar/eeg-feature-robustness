\begin{abstract}
\noindent
EEG-based brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) frequently report high within-dataset
classification accuracy, yet performance collapses when models are applied to new
datasets recorded under different conditions. This paper investigates whether
frequency-domain feature representations are more robust than time-domain
statistical features under cross-dataset distribution shift. Using two publicly
available motor imagery datasets---PhysioNet EEG Motor Imagery (109 subjects)
and BCI Competition IV Dataset~2a (9 subjects)---we evaluate 9 feature-model
combinations under strict zero-shot transfer in both directions. FFT magnitude
features showed the smallest mean normalised generalisation gap (0.065), followed
by band power (0.070) and time-domain features (0.071), though differences were
modest. Critically, within-dataset accuracy was near chance for PhysioNet (53--56\%),
indicating that commonly reported high within-dataset accuracies do not
necessarily reflect genuine cross-dataset generalisation. A $9\times$ amplitude difference between recording
systems was empirically confirmed as the primary barrier to cross-dataset transfer;
log-transformed frequency features partially mitigate this shift.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) based on electroencephalography (EEG) have shown
considerable promise for enabling motor-impaired individuals to communicate through
imagined movement. Motor imagery---the mental rehearsal of a motor action---produces
characteristic suppression of the mu (8--12~Hz) and beta (13--30~Hz) rhythms over
the contralateral motor cortex, providing a reliable neural signal for binary
classification.

Despite decades of research, EEG-based BCIs face a persistent barrier: models
trained on one dataset rarely transfer to another. Studies routinely report
within-dataset accuracies of 80--90\%~\cite{wang2020,mathiyazhagan2025}, yet these numbers are produced by training
and testing on the same subjects under the same recording conditions. When the same
model is applied to a different dataset---different subjects, amplifiers, electrode
configurations---performance collapses to near chance. This gap between reported
and real-world accuracy is a fundamental challenge for clinical BCI deployment.

The causes are multiple: electrode impedance and amplifier gain differences
introduce amplitude-scale shifts; different subject pools introduce inter-subject
variability; different experimental protocols introduce trial-timing differences.
What remains underexplored is \emph{which feature representation} degrades least
under these conditions, and why.

This paper addresses that question directly. We compare three classical feature
representations---time-domain statistics, band power, and FFT magnitude---under
strict zero-shot cross-dataset transfer. Using classical machine learning
deliberately isolates the feature representation as the variable of interest.
Deep learning conflates feature extraction with classification, making it
impossible to attribute generalisation differences to the representation itself.

Our central hypothesis is that frequency-domain features will be more robust under
distribution shift than time-domain features, because EEG's oscillatory band
structure is more stable across recording conditions than its absolute amplitude.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0pt, topsep=3pt]
  \item A controlled empirical comparison of three classical feature representations
    under strict cross-dataset transfer in both directions.
  \item Empirical quantification of the amplitude-scale distribution shift between
    two widely used public datasets.
  \item A transparent within-dataset accuracy characterisation for an unscreened
    subject population, providing a realistic pooled trial-wise baseline for
    interpreting cross-dataset robustness.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Motor Imagery Classification}

The classification of motor imagery EEG has been studied since Pfurtscheller and
Neuper~\cite{pfurtscheller2001} established the neurophysiological basis of
event-related desynchronisation in the mu and beta bands. Standard approaches
extract band-power features using Welch's PSD estimation and classify with linear
discriminant analysis or SVMs. Common spatial patterns (CSP) and its variants
have been widely applied. Within-dataset accuracies of 70--90\% are regularly
reported, particularly on competition benchmarks such as BCI Competition IV
Dataset~2a.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Generalisation}

Cross-dataset generalisation is significantly less studied than within-dataset
evaluation. Jayaram et al.~\cite{jayaram2016} demonstrated through the MOABB
benchmark that many published BCI algorithms show dramatic performance drops
under cross-session and cross-subject evaluation~\cite{zhu2025,ingolfsson2020}, explicitly framing this as a
\emph{cross-dataset variability problem} that weakens generalisation.

Crucially, the part of the literature that most directly addresses ``robustness
across datasets'' has mostly targeted deep-learning and domain adaptation approaches:
recent reviews of MI-EEG classification are dominated by deep architectures
\cite{schirrmeister2017}, while contemporary cross-dataset MI work centres on
transfer learning, pre-training, covariate-shift alignment, and graph-based
models~\cite{lotte2018}. Classical feature-engineering work exists but is almost
exclusively evaluated \emph{within} a single dataset~\cite{padfield2019}.

\subsection{Gap This Study Fills}

To our knowledge, prior work has rarely isolated feature representation as the
\emph{sole} variable under a strict train-on-$A$, test-on-$B$ design with
identical preprocessing and both transfer directions. Existing work more often
focuses on domain adaptation, deep architectures, or cross-subject evaluation
within a single dataset. Our study provides a controlled classical baseline
against which such approaches can be compared.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Datasets}

\textbf{PhysioNet EEG Motor Imagery}~\cite{schalk2004,goldberger2000} comprises
recordings from 109 subjects at 160~Hz on 64 channels. Only the left/right motor
imagery runs (runs~0, 1, and 2 in MOABB notation) were retained. After
preprocessing, 4,918 trials were kept (2,480 left, 2,438 right).

\textbf{BCI Competition IV Dataset~2a}~\cite{brunner2008}, accessed as
\texttt{BNCI2014\_001} via MOABB~\cite{jayaram2018}, comprises recordings from
9 subjects at 250~Hz on 22 EEG channels. Only left-hand (event~769) and
right-hand (event~770) trials were retained, yielding 2,592 trials (1,296 each).

\subsection{Preprocessing}

An identical pipeline was applied to both datasets:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=3pt, label=\arabic*.]
  \item \textbf{Channel selection}: both recordings restricted to the 22 BCI2a
    channels (a strict subset of PhysioNet's 64). No interpolation required.
  \item \textbf{Resampling}: PhysioNet upsampled from 160~Hz to 250~Hz using MNE
    polyphase resampling. Upsampling was preferred to preserve all frequency content
    below the original Nyquist (80~Hz).
  \item \textbf{Bandpass filtering}: 4--40~Hz FIR filter; captures the
    motor-relevant mu and beta rhythms while removing DC drift and EMG artefacts.
  \item \textbf{Epoch extraction}: 0.5--2.5~s post-cue; the 0.5~s offset avoids
    the cue-evoked visual potential. No baseline correction applied (bandpass
    renders epoch means approximately zero).
  \item \textbf{Label encoding}: left~$= 0$, right~$= 1$.
\end{enumerate}
Output arrays have shape $(\text{n\_trials},\, 22,\, 501)$.

\subsection{Feature Extraction}

\textbf{Feature A --- Time Domain (44-dim).} Mean and variance per channel
over time. Because the signal is bandpass-filtered, means are approximately
zero; variance is the primary discriminative signal. This set is intentionally
simple---it tests the weakest possible time-domain representation, not
time-domain features in general.

\textbf{Feature B --- Band Power (88-dim).} Welch PSD (nperseg~$=256$, 50\%
overlap, Hann window) per channel; average power in delta (0.5--4~Hz), theta
(4--8~Hz), alpha (8--13~Hz), and beta (13--30~Hz). Natural log transform applied
to normalise the skewed distribution and compress amplitude differences across
recording systems. Yields $22\times4=88$ features.

\textbf{Feature C --- FFT Magnitude (792-dim).} FFT magnitude restricted to
4--40~Hz, linearly interpolated to 36 fixed frequency bins per channel. No log
transform; StandardScaler inside the pipeline handles scale. Linear interpolation
(over cubic spline) was chosen to preserve sharp spectral peaks. Yields
$22\times36=792$ features.

\subsection{Classifiers}

Three classifiers were evaluated in scikit-learn Pipelines:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Logistic Regression}: L2 regularisation, max\_iter~$=5000$,
    preceded by StandardScaler.
  \item \textbf{SVM}: RBF kernel ($C{=}1$, gamma~$=\texttt{scale}$), preceded by
    StandardScaler.
  \item \textbf{Random Forest}: 100 trees; no feature scaling (scale-invariant).
\end{itemize}
Placing StandardScaler inside the Pipeline ensures it is fitted only on training
data, preventing leakage.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

\textbf{Within-dataset} evaluation used 5-fold stratified cross-validation
(shuffle, random\_state~$=42$). Because trials were pooled across subjects
before splitting, these scores should be interpreted as pooled trial-wise
baselines rather than fully subject-independent grouped-subject estimates.

\textbf{Cross-dataset} evaluation used zero-shot transfer: train on the full
source dataset, test on the full target dataset. Both directions were evaluated:
PhysioNet~$\to$~BCI2a and BCI2a~$\to$~PhysioNet.

Primary metric: \emph{balanced accuracy} (mean per-class recall), robust to
class imbalance. All experiments used seed~42.

The normalised generalisation gap is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Norm.\ Gap} = \frac{BA_{\text{within}} - BA_{\text{cross}}}{BA_{\text{within}}}
  \label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}
An interpretation guide adapted from cross-dataset MI literature:
gap~$<0.10$ is small degradation; 0.10--0.20 is moderate; $>0.20$ is severe.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Accuracy and Generalisation Gap}

Table~\ref{tab:cross} and Figure~\ref{fig:gap} report cross-dataset results.
The two transfer directions showed strikingly different patterns.

\textbf{PhysioNet~$\to$~BCI2a}: gaps were near zero or negative across all
feature sets. Logistic Regression achieved slightly \emph{higher} cross-dataset
than within-dataset accuracy on PhysioNet, producing negative gaps---a consequence
of the near-chance PhysioNet baseline rather than genuine transfer.

\textbf{BCI2a~$\to$~PhysioNet}: gaps were substantially larger (0.12--0.17),
representing \emph{moderate} degradation by the interpretation guide. This
reflects genuine performance loss when a model trained on 9 subjects is tested
on 109 from a different recording system.

Averaged across all models and both directions, FFT showed the smallest mean
normalised gap (0.065), followed by Band Power (0.070) and Time Domain (0.071).
Logistic Regression was the most robust model (mean gap: 0.059). All values
fall in the \emph{small} degradation range, though the BCI~$\to$~Phys direction
alone is consistently moderate.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Amplitude Distribution Shift}

A diagnostic analysis of preprocessed arrays revealed a $9\times$ amplitude
difference between datasets: PhysioNet signals ranged ${\pm}856\,\mu$V while
BCI2a ranged ${\pm}96\,\mu$V. This arises from different recording hardware,
electrode impedance, and amplifier gain---not from any difference in the
underlying neural activity.

This is the primary mechanistic explanation for cross-dataset degradation.
Time-domain variance features are proportional to amplitude squared, making
them maximally sensitive. FFT magnitude features scale linearly with amplitude.
Log-transformed band power compresses the scale difference: empirically, despite
the $9\times$ amplitude gap, log band power means were nearly identical across
datasets (PhysioNet: $-22.8$, BCI2a: $-23.0$).

\subsection{Why FFT Outperformed Band Power}

The hypothesis predicted band power as most robust, but FFT showed a slightly
smaller average gap. Log-transformed band power demonstrably compresses the
amplitude shift, yet FFT's finer 36-bin resolution retains discriminative
spectral information that broad band averages lose. The StandardScaler inside
the FFT pipeline normalises scale at training time, partially compensating for
amplitude differences without the coarsening effect of band aggregation.

\subsection{The Near-Chance PhysioNet Baseline}

Within-dataset accuracy on PhysioNet was near chance (51--56\%) across all
configurations. This is not a pipeline error: run verification confirmed correct
trial selection; signal diagnostics confirmed valid EEG-range amplitudes. The
near-chance result reflects the genuine difficulty of motor imagery classification
on an unscreened, heterogeneous 109-subject pool using zero-calibration classical
features.

This has a direct implication for the literature. Reported accuracies of
80--90\%~\cite{wang2020,mathiyazhagan2025} on similar paradigms may reflect differences in evaluation protocol,
subject selection, or subject-specific calibration rather than population-level
cross-dataset generalisation~\cite{jayaram2018}. In this study, the within-dataset scores should
be interpreted as pooled trial-wise cross-validation baselines rather than
fully subject-independent population-level estimates, because trials from the
same subject can appear in different folds.

\subsection{The Asymmetric Transfer Directions}

The two transfer directions told fundamentally different stories.
Phys~$\to$~BCI showed near-zero or negative gaps---not because transfer
worked, but because the PhysioNet baseline was already at chance.
BCI~$\to$~Phys revealed moderate degradation of 12--17\%, as 9 training
subjects cannot capture the distributional variance of 109 test subjects
from a different recording system. A study reporting only one transfer
direction risks deeply misleading conclusions.

\subsection{Logistic Regression as Most Robust Model}

Logistic Regression showed the smallest average generalisation gap (0.059).
This is consistent with the principle that simpler models generalise better
under distribution shift: Random Forest can overfit to dataset-specific
patterns during training, amplifying degradation when those patterns are
absent in the target.

\section{Limitations}

\textbf{Two datasets only.} Findings are based on two datasets with similar
left/right hand imagery paradigms. Generalisation to other EEG tasks (P300,
SSVEP, cognitive load) or more diverse hardware cannot be assumed.

\textbf{Near-chance PhysioNet baseline.} Near-chance within-dataset accuracy
on PhysioNet severely limits interpretability of the Phys~$\to$~BCI direction:
a meaningful generalisation gap requires a baseline sufficiently above chance.

\textbf{Intentionally simple time-domain features.} The time-domain family
uses only per-channel mean and variance. This is an intentionally weak baseline
that tests simple statistical time-domain features, not time-domain
representations in general. Richer time-domain families (e.g., Hjorth
parameters, zero-crossing rates, AR coefficients) exist and may show different
robustness characteristics.

\textbf{Classical models only.} Deep learning may show qualitatively different
generalisation patterns. This study deliberately excludes deep architectures to
isolate feature representation effects; findings should not be extrapolated.

\textbf{No domain adaptation.} Results represent strict zero-shot transfer.
This study establishes the unadapted baseline against which alignment and
adaptation methods should be compared.

\textbf{Single random seed.} Cross-dataset experiments used a single seed.
Within-dataset results include fold-level variance; cross-dataset point
estimates lack confidence intervals.

\textbf{No subject-level analysis.} Population-level near-chance accuracy may
mask a bimodal distribution of strong and weak motor imagery responders.
Subject-level analysis remains for future work.

\section{Conclusion}

This study provides a controlled empirical comparison of three classical EEG
feature representations---time-domain statistics, log-transformed band power,
and FFT magnitude---under strict zero-shot cross-dataset transfer between two
publicly available motor imagery datasets. FFT features showed the smallest
mean normalised generalisation gap (0.065), followed by band power (0.070) and
time-domain features (0.071), with Logistic Regression as the most robust
classifier. A $9\times$ amplitude difference between recording systems was
identified as the primary mechanistic source of distribution shift.
Log-transformed band power partially mitigates this shift, but differences
between feature sets were modest, suggesting no classical representation fully
solves the cross-dataset transfer problem. Within-dataset accuracy on an
unscreened 109-subject population was near chance (51--56\%) and, in this
study, should be interpreted as a pooled trial-wise baseline rather than a
fully subject-independent estimate. These findings highlight the importance of
bidirectional cross-dataset evaluation as a standard practice and motivate
future work on amplitude-invariant feature representations for robust neural
decoding.
